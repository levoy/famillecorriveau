Marie-Josephte "The Corriveau"

There has never been any woman with such a bad reputation and of whom many authors have written in the New France papers. Historians and story tellers alike have made her the greatest murderer of all times. It has been pretended that she had killed seven husbands as well as being a sorcerer with devilish powers.

In fact, she was only an innocent victim who was at the wrong place, at the wrong time.

You will read hereafter the true story of Marie-Josephte.

She was born in Saint-Vallier, May 14, 1733, daughter of Joseph Corriveau and Marie-Francoise Bolduc. She is the second born of nine children and the only one who lived to an adult age.

At 16 years of age, she weds Charles Bouchard, a farmer in Saint-Vallier, on November 15, 1749. He is the son of Nicolas Bouchard and Anne Sylvain. They have three children: Francoise, Angelique and Charles Junior. In the spring of 1760, Charles came back home after the conquest of Quebec by the English army. It is said that he did not take defeat well, fell sick and died of yellow fever in April of the same year. He was burried in the Saint-Vallier cemetery on April 27.

The widow is now free to be wood by all good parties of surrounding villages as she is a well appreciated woman. She choses Louis Dodier without knowing that he would be at the origin of all stories that have fascinated America over the ages. So she weds July 20, 1761. He is the son of Pierre Dodier and Marie Lebrun.

This union does not seem to be a success from the beginning and stories soon start spreading. Some say he is jealous and violent. Others say that she is frivolous and likes men in red uniforms. What is important is that he is found dead in his stable on the morning of January 27, 1763, apparently stamped to death by his horses.

She is arrested for this crime with her father who is accused of murder and she of collusion. After a first court action, the father is condemned to be hung and she is condemned to be whipped sixty times on her bare back as well as her left hand marked with a red iron with the letter M for her collusion in the murder.

The unpredicted testimony of a priest will reverse the judgment. Pere Augustin-Louis Glapion, who is at the time the superior of the Jesuite community will testify that he had heard the confession of the father and that he was innocent, that the daughter is the only one responsible for the death of her husband.

The father is released and Marie-Josephte condemned to hang and left hanging at the crossroad of Pointe a Levis for everyone to view. Her sentence was executed on April 18, 1763 on Buttes-a-Nepveu, near Plaines d'Abraham in Quebec City. Her father was forgiven of this crime by royal decree of Georges 111 August 8, 1763.

It would be too cumbersome to relate all that has transpired in this famous court action. I will only say that documents of both court actions have been discovered in London in 1947, which documents have allowed facts to be righted.

She was not the sorcerer legends had her and her destiny remains a tragedy.

In 1989, the Quebec bar association has studied all information received from London in 1947 about the court actions of this famous person belonging to our history. All people involved in the reconstitution of this story have come to the same conclusion: nothing in the court papers proves in any way that she was guilty of murder. So, two hundred and twenty-six years later, she is declared innocent.
